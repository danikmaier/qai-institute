  # Towards a Rooted Modernism

  We are thirty years into independence, and Kazakh architecture remains caught between two failed projects: the Soviet inheritance of universalist modernism that denied local specificity, and the nationalist kitsch that reduces a complex spatial tradition to the yurt silhouette stamped onto every building regardless of programme or context.

  Both failures share a common root: the belief that architecture's relationship to culture is primarily a matter of surface. The Soviet modernist suppressed ornament; the nationalist decorator added it back. Neither engaged with the deeper question: what does it mean to build in a place with this climate, this geology, this social structure, this history of habitation?

  ## The Climate Question

  Kazakhstan spans eleven climate zones. Almaty's mild mountain climate, Astana's continental extremes (-40°C to +40°C), and the Aral Sea basin's desiccated post-ecological landscape each demand radically different architectural responses. Traditional Kazakh building knowledge — embodied in the yurt's insulation logic, in the deep-shaded aiwan of southern settlements, in the earth construction of the Syr Darya valley — was climate knowledge. It was architecture as metabolic response.

  Contemporary Kazakh architecture, dominated by glass-and-steel towers imported wholesale from warmer climates, ignores this knowledge entirely. The result is buildings that are energetically catastrophic and spatially alienating.

  ## The Material Question

  The steppe contains extraordinary material intelligence. The felt-making tradition, the carved wood working of the Altai region, the fired brick and tile ceramics of the south — these are not decorative traditions but structural knowledge systems developed over centuries of working with specific materials in specific conditions.

  A genuinely Kazakh modernism would begin with materials. Not as surface application but as structural logic. What would a building look like that took the thermal mass of rammed earth seriously? That applied the geometric intelligence of Kazakh ornament to structural patterning rather than decorative appliqué?

  ## A Proposal

  The architects I admire most are those who have accepted the difficulty of this problem rather than resolving it prematurely. They work slowly, building from the ground up — literally — starting with soil tests and climate data rather than imported typologies. They are willing to produce buildings that look unlike anything before, because a genuine synthesis between modernist construction methods and Kazakh spatial intelligence has never been attempted in good faith.

  The QAI Institute exists to support this work. We document what has been built, analyse what failed and why, and create the conditions for a genuinely new Kazakh architecture to emerge — one that is neither nostalgic nor globally generic, but specifically and proudly of this place.
