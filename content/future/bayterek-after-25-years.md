  # Bayterek at Twenty-Five

  The Bayterek tower was completed in 2002 and immediately became the visual symbol of Kazakhstan's new capital. Designed by Norman Foster's office in collaboration with President Nazarbayev, who reportedly sketched the original concept — a tree holding a golden egg — on a napkin, it is perhaps the most literal piece of symbolic architecture produced in the post-Soviet space.

  Twenty-five years on, it is worth asking what Bayterek has given to Astana's urban life, and what it has taken away.

  ## What it gave

  Bayterek gave Astana an identity. In a city built from scratch on a featureless steppe, a legible symbol was not a luxury but a necessity. The tower's silhouette appears on postage stamps, football jerseys, and wedding photographs. It has become genuinely popular — not in the ironic, architectural-tourism sense, but in the way that the Eiffel Tower is popular: as a place where people go to mark occasions and feel something about where they live.

  ## What it cost

  The problem with Bayterek is not the building itself but the urban model it inaugurated. Its success as a symbol encouraged a conception of city-building as the accumulation of iconic set-pieces — each building a logo rather than a piece of urban fabric. The result is a city that photographs well from a helicopter but functions poorly at street level.

  Astana's boulevards are scaled for pageantry, not for pedestrians. The space between the buildings — which is where urban life actually happens — was an afterthought.

  ## The lesson

  The lesson of Bayterek is not that symbolic architecture is wrong. Kazakhstan needed symbols. The lesson is that symbols cannot substitute for urban design. The next twenty-five years of Astana should be devoted not to adding more icons but to repairing the spaces between them — to making the city liveable at the scale of the human body, not the scale of the presidential motorcade.
