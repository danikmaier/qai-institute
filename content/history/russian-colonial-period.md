description_en: |
  The Russian imperial expansion into the steppe from the 1820s onward introduced a grid-plan urbanism that was fundamentally alien to nomadic spatial traditions. Fort cities — Verniy (later Alma-Ata, later Almaty), Petropavlovsk, Semipalatinsk — were laid out on military-administrative logic, with Russian Orthodox churches, governor's residences, and barracks as architectural anchors.

  The 1911 earthquake that destroyed much of Verniy accelerated the transition to reinforced concrete construction and contributed to the adoption of a distinctive regional architectural style that combined Russian provincial classicism with early modernist influences.

  The early Soviet period (1917–1953) produced an intense architectural experiment: from the brief flourishing of constructivism in the 1920s to the imposition of Socialist Realism in the 1930s, and finally to the monumental Stalinist classicism of the postwar reconstruction period. The architectural character of Almaty's central districts was fundamentally shaped in this era.
