description_en: |
  The Khrushchev Thaw inaugurated the most radical urban transformation Kazakhstan had seen. The industrial prefabrication of the khrushchyovka housing blocks enabled the rapid expansion of Alma-Ata, Karaganda, and the new capital Tselinograd (now Astana), housing millions of workers arriving for the Virgin Lands campaign.

  Simultaneously, a generation of Kazakh architects educated in Leningrad and Moscow returned to develop a sophisticated local modernism that engaged creatively with the tension between internationalist modernist doctrine and the Khrushchev decree requiring "national character" in architecture. This produced inventive buildings that incorporated Kazakh ornamental elements into modernist structural grids — a formal problem that remains unresolved and productive.

  The 1966 Tashkent earthquake and the subsequent reconstruction debate resonated strongly in Kazakhstan, accelerating seismic-resistant design research and the development of distinctive construction typologies adapted to Central Asian climate and soil conditions.
