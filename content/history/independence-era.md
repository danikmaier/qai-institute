description_en: |
  Kazakhstan's independence in 1991 triggered an unprecedented architectural project: the construction of an entirely new capital. The decision to move the capital from Almaty to the northern steppe city of Akmola (renamed Astana, then Nur-Sultan, now Astana again) created a tabula rasa for national architectural expression that attracted the world's most celebrated starchitects.

  Norman Foster, Kisho Kurokawa, Manfredi Nicoletti, and others were commissioned to design major civic buildings, producing a skyline of deliberate spectacle. The Bayterek tower, the Khan Shatyr entertainment centre, the Palace of Peace and Reconciliation, and the Abu Dhabi Plaza — each building announced Kazakhstan's sovereign ambitions on the world stage.

  This era also produced a significant backlash: Kazakh architects and cultural historians began arguing for a more rooted architecture that engaged with local climate, materials, and spatial traditions rather than importing international spectacle. This debate defines the current state of Kazakh architecture and is central to the mission of the QAI Institute.
