abstract_en: |
  This paper examines the spatial and structural logic of the traditional Kazakh yurt (kiiz üy) as a precedent for contemporary architectural thinking. Moving beyond the yurt as folkloric object, we argue that its principles — radial organisation, demountability, hierarchical spatial zoning, and the relationship between structural performance and ornamental surface — constitute a coherent design philosophy with direct relevance to contemporary sustainable architecture.

  Through case studies of three contemporary Kazakh architects who have explicitly engaged with yurt logic, we demonstrate that the most productive translations are those that operate at the level of spatial organisation rather than formal mimicry. The paper proposes a taxonomy of appropriation modes — symbolic, structural, and spatial — and evaluates each against criteria of cultural authenticity and architectural integrity.
