abstract_en: |
  The Khrushchev Thaw and subsequent decades produced a distinctive strain of modernist architecture across Soviet Kazakhstan that has been largely overlooked in western architectural historiography. This paper reconstructs the institutional framework, key protagonists, and formal programmes of Kazakh Soviet modernism, arguing that its negotiation between universalist modernist principles and localised national form-giving constitutes one of the most complex and productive episodes in twentieth-century architecture.

  Drawing on newly accessible archive material from AlmatyGiprogor, oral histories with surviving practitioners, and close formal analysis of thirty-eight significant buildings, the paper traces three distinct phases: the Thaw period experimentation (1955–1965), mature Socialist realism's decline and the rise of panel construction (1965–1980), and the late Soviet period's renewed interest in national ornament (1980–1991).
